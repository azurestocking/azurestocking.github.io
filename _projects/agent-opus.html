Part 01

Overview

Opus Clip is a Series B startup specializing in AI-powered short video repurposing and captioning — but when this project began, they were pre-Series A, exploring how to integrate text-to-video generation into their existing product.

Our research yielded data-driven insights through iterative prototype-based testing, uncovering diverse user segments and their varied expectations and shared high-level goals, culminating in a design proposal for an end-to-end, human-AI collaborative storytelling support tool.

Background

Back in Q2 2024, our company saw text-to-video integration as both timely and technically feasible, but this potentially valuable yet loosely defined opportunity needed to be validated and conceptualized quickly and affordably, before securing leadership buy-in and diverting core production efforts.

Insight #1
Market Interest

Advances in generative AI have fueled momentum in multimodal creation, with text-to-video emerging as a compelling frontier. Early movers like Pika and Runway signal both technical feasibility and strong market demand.

Insight #2
Existential Crisis

Our company is among the earliest players in AI-powered video captioning and repurposing, but the low barrier to entry makes differentiation essential to maintain its competitive edge as more industry giants like ByteDance, YouTube, Meta enter the space.

Insight #3
Potential Synergy

Existing users have been requesting text-to-video generation, a capability that naturally complements the current product by extending the workflow earlier in the pipeline.

Challenges

Brought in as an external consultant, I led a focused rapid prototyping sprint under low internal priority, where the core challenge was not just delivering the design, but resolving the strategic ambiguity beneath it.

Insight #1
Limited Resources

With Series A fundraising underway and a lean team stretched thin, there was no bandwidth for a full-time hire, concept testing had to be fast and lightweight, leaving me with limited resources and visibility.

Insight #2
Twofold Ambiguity

The research needed to work on two fronts: strategically, to validate the opportunity by defining a value proposition and identifying target user segments; and tactically, to translate that into a feature design aligned with real user workflows.

Problem Statement

We began with a straightforward, feature-driven problem framing handed down by the product manager, simple by necessity, but knowingly incomplete:

How might we integrate a text-to-video generating feature into the existing short video repurposing pipeline?

As later research surfaced three high-level goals shared across two user segments — efficiency, consistency, and adaptability — I refined that rough question into something more specific, which guided the design decisions that follow:

How might we empower content creators to quickly and easily translate their scripts into reliably high-quality videos while accommodating diverse scenarios?

Design Process

We adopted a Lean approach to move quickly and stay focused on user needs, using iterative prototyping to validate assumptions and evaluate outcomes, thereby enabling confident product direction without overcommitting resources.

Highlights

Proposed an end-to-end storytelling support tool for AI video production, built on a multistage generative media pipeline focused on human-AI collaborative workflow enhancement rather than dependence on powerful models, enabling faster iteration, consistent output, and flexible control.

Part 02

Think, Make, Check

Starting with a hypothesis: leading players appeared to focus on generating impressive but disjointed video clips, placing less emphasis on connecting them into structured narrative, which positions themselves more as tools for visual experimentation and asset creation.

Market Analysis

To test this hypothesis, I identified several leading players across the production cycle and established a set of criteria to evaluate their narrative capabilities.

The matrix reveals a strategic opportunity: to differentiate by centering on storytelling support, aligning with the market gap and the team’s strengths, instead of competing on model performance.

Brainstorm

We brainstormed features that would support storytelling more directly than model-centric competitors, using a mind map. Those deemed technically feasible and within design scope were marked as low-hanging fruits.

Wireframe

We explored how to organize the feature set through concrete interface paradigms and interaction patterns, arriving at two different directions.

Option #1
Storyboard

A sequential visual planning tool widely used in film and animation to pre-visualize productions, as seen in tools like Toon Boom Storyboard.

Option #2
Infinite Canvas

A modular approach used by domain experts to design and execute complex systems visually, such as Grasshopper and Unreal Engine.

Concept Testing

To assess which direction had the strategic and technical foundation to move forward, I facilitated a cross-functional stakeholder interview to walk through annotated sketches and evaluate its viability, grouping the responses into thematic categories.

Part 03

Think, Make, Check +1

Despite the node-based canvas being an intriguing direction, particularly for designers and engineers, the findings told a different story, raising concerns significant enough to rule it out.

Data Analysis

Synthesizing the responses, the affinity mapping revealed that implementing a node-based architecture would require substantial engineering refactoring, raising critical concerns across three dimensions:

Insight #1
Technical Complexity

Each cell would be developed standalone yet communicative, and the magnitude of potential cell combinations exponentially poses challenges in scalability, agility, and version control.

Insight #2
Usability Uncertainty

The added flexibility introduces a higher risk of choice paralysis and cognitive overload, potentially alienating users with a fragmented, unpredictable, and slow experience.

Insight #3
Managerial Misalignment

Building and maintaining this system demands significant investment in labor, capital, and time, causing real risk of delays, scope shifts, or failure to meet long-term strategic goals.

Given these constraints and an unfavorable input-output ratio, we deprioritized the node-based direction and redirected focus toward the storyboard alternative.

Prototype

We introduced an intermediary step of text-to-image generation to stabilize the creative workflow, enabling users to preview keyframes and iterate on their sequence, timing, and narrative logic in areas where models typically fall short.

Option #1
Prompt

Enrich user input with storytelling techniques to produce a complete, engaging screenplay.

Screenplay

Parse the screenplay into shot-by-shot scripts with structured data to guide text-to-image generation.

Storyboard

Tweak keyframes, voiceover, and animation settings to better align with the user’s creative intent.

Slideshow

Simulate the structure and pacing of the final video to reduce uncertainty in image-to-video generation.

Usability Testing

To test the prototype across hypothesized user segments, I recruited a slightly larger sample of 8 participants via a screener survey, capturing how each might integrate the feature into their existing workflow. Sessions employed the think-aloud protocol to surface usability and functionality issues in real time.

Responses were synthesized through affinity mapping, grouped thematically and color-coded by usage intensity, to surface areas for improvement and drive the next iteration.

Part 04

Think, Make, Check +2

Digging deeper, something more interesting emerged — users were converging on the same problems while diverging sharply on how to solve them, which raises a more fundamental question: why do users hold conflicting opinions on the same areas for improvement?

Data Analysis

To make sense of these apparent contradictions, I hypothesized that the conflicting needs stemmed from how users conceptualize a set of shared goals differently.

To test this, I examined the underlying intent behind each request and reshuffled them by high-level goals — the broader needs that encompass divergent user behaviors and expectations.

The affinity mapping supports this hypothesis: despite appearing contradictory at the surface level, all requests consistently mapped to the same high-level goals, indicating that users differ in their expectations about how these goals should be achieved rather than holding fundamentally incompatible needs, and are therefore reconcilable.

Goal #1
Efficiency

Reduce time, effort, financial cost, and cognitive friction by providing readable information, an intuitive interface, and a streamlined, user-friendly workflow.

Goal #2
Consistency

Enable a coherent narrative and stable visual identity across frames, while allowing reasonable variation to support continuity in action, time, and space.

Goal #3
Adaptability

Support varied intents and use cases through rich customization options, flexible control over outputs and workflows, and a context-aware, responsive system.

This insight validates our hypothesis of multiple user segments, and the divergence in expectations implies that distinct user groups adopt varying positions on value trade-offs, potentially making them visually identifiable when plotted on a coordinate plane defined by such trade-offs.

To surface those groups, I identified the core tensions in users’ decision-making: users are primarily weighing cost against perceived quality, and user agency against automation.

Insight #1
Cost vs. Perceived Quality

This axis plots users’ expectations for production by reflecting their weighting of resources commitment against the value of outcomes. Dimensions highlighted in feedback include time-saved, effort-saved, and craftsmanship, with the extremes of this axis representing cost and perceived quality.

Insight #2
User Agency vs. Automation

This axis plots users’ preferences for interaction by reflecting their willingness to delegate creative control to the system. Dimensions highlighted in feedback include authorship, precision, and freedom, with the extremes of this axis representing user agency versus automation.

Mapping all sticky notes along the axes representing the two core tensions above revealed the natural clustering of user segments.

Sticky notes were distributed diagonally from the upper-left to lower-right quadrants, with high and low usage intensity clustering at opposite ends, demonstrating that:

Insight #1
We Don’t Trust AI

The negative correlation between perceived quality and automation validates the hypothesis that users associate strong outputs with manual control, implying limited trust in fully automated systems to deliver professional results.

Insight #2
User Segmentation

The polarization by usage intensity reveals two distinct user groups: those with lower intensity favoring low cost and automation, and others with higher intensity prioritizing user agency and quality.

Personae

Based on the user segmentation, I developed the following two personae: original creators are typically users with a high level of creative expertise, such as indie filmmakers and animators; derivative creators are more likely users with distribution-driven priorities, including influencers and content aggregators.

Persona #1
Original Creator

Seek to maintain creative authorship while achieving high-quality outputs; cautious of full automation and perceive the tool as an extension.

Persona #2
Derivative Creator

Aim to produce acceptable outputs with minimal input; willing to delegate complex tasks to the system and view the tool as a replacement.

Prototype

We improved clarity and workflow efficiency through both structural and functional enhancements, including a redesigned layout, surfaced global settings, and centralized asset control to support faster, more consistent generation.

Iteration #1

Layout Efficiency

BEFORE

Shots were arranged horizontally, with the container and thumbnail slider lengths misaligned, causing visual inconsistency. Also, this left users uncertain whether to scroll horizontally or vertically, especially on touch screens.

AFTER

Switched to a vertical layout with thumbnail and editor modes, aligning with standard browsing behavior. Shot editing options are collapsed behind hover effects to reduce cognitive load and display only relevant information.

Iteration #2

Customization Efficiency Consistency

BEFORE

Global settings were configured via an inconspicuous icon in the corner, leading to repetitive manual setup each time and frequent oversights in maintaining consistent output quality.

AFTER

Exposed global settings in a prominent sidebar for constant visibility, and introduced predefined and customizable templates to streamline setup and ensure output consistency.

BEFORE

Each shot need to be tweaked manually to maintain visual consistency, a time-consuming, error-prone process that scaled poorly across long productions.

AFTER

Extracted shared assets (character, scene) from the script automatically, allowing users to insert and apply them across multiple shots at once.

Iteration #3

Inline Editing Efficiency Consistency

BEFORE

AI-assisted text enhancements were applied to one shot’s entire text area, indiscriminately altering unrelated content and lacking support for targeted control.

AFTER

Introduced a floating toolbar that activates on text selection, allowing users to apply predefined functions (polish, shorten, and regenerate) or instruction-based prompts to specific segments only.

Iteration #4

Separate Workflow Adaptability

BEFORE

The workflow followed a fixed linear path that assumes all users start from scratch and proceed through every stage, adding friction for more experienced or prepared users.

AFTER

Introduced conditional branching and stage-skip options that allow users to bypass unnecessary stages mid-flow based on their readiness, reducing the minimum required steps.

Usability Testing

The updated prototype underwent testing with 5 additional participants, and their responses were grouped by thematic categories to identify areas for improvement.

Part 05

Think, Make, Check +3

The testing pointed to new areas for improvement, but a closer look at the responses revealed something unexpected: several responses didn’t fit any known category of high-level goals, surfacing an unstated one.

Data Analysis

Even with their diverse focuses, the uncategorized feedback unanimously converged on a previously unmentioned dimension: transparency — a deeper understanding of the system’s operations and decisions, and hence more informed and targeted manipulation.

Prototype

We incorporated transparency as a new evaluation lens while continuing to improve core usability through fine-grained refinements, including domain-specific text enhancement, clarified interface hierarchy and interaction logic, and more informative iteration support.

Iteration #1

Script Reformatting Efficiency Consistency

BEFORE

Scripts were composed in plain text with minimal markdown-style grammar for basic formatting, confusing non-technical users.

AFTER

Added a reformat option that declares key elements with symbolic tags, improving both frontend readability and model performance.

BEFORE

All function buttons displayed both icons and text labels, then revealed an additional slider to adjust tone or length, creating redundant choices and unnecessary clicks.

AFTER

Replaced function controls with icon buttons, visually distinguished them from open-ended prompt input to reflect different interaction modes, and simplified predefined options to remove redundant two-step interactions.

Iteration #2

Shot Editing Consistency Adaptability Transparency

BEFORE

Controls suffered from an illogical information architecture: unrelated functions juxtaposed and parallel options incorrectly nested. An overuse of varied UI components made the interface even more disorienting.

AFTER

Regrouped controls by their target objects and logical relationships, distributing them between the floating toolbar and modal accordingly, consolidated UI components to a succinct set.

BEFORE

Clicking a trigger button navigated users to a separate page to view the output keyframe, breaking the traceable connection between input and result with no visual continuity between the two.

AFTER

Flattened the page hierarchy using modals and in-textarea dropdowns, keeping input and output in close proximity to establish a more intuitive cause-and-effect relationship.

BEFORE

Audio tracks were generated exclusively from voiceover input, limiting the tool to genres like vlogs and commentary where narration closely follows the visuals.

AFTER

Added a dedicated dialogue track separate from voiceover, allowing each shot to carry both independently, broadening support to formats like feature films where audio and visuals frequently diverge.

Iteration #3

Preview Transparency

BEFORE

Preview was only accessible at the final step, with no option to initiate it mid-production, discouraging early identification of issues.

AFTER

Introduced a slideshow mode that simulates the final output’s pacing and structure, accessible at any stage with fluid switching between modes.

Iteration #4

Version Control Transparency

BEFORE

Every generation attempt was saved indiscriminately, creating a cluttered, undifferentiated list that offered little value for tracking meaningful changes or refining prompts.

AFTER

Replaced the flat version history with a more purposeful iteration log that highlights only meaningful differences relative to the last selected version — giving users a clearer basis for informed, targeted revisions.

Success Metrics

Full implementation would be a major, resource-intensive undertaking requiring leadership buy-in and broader organizational alignment — none of which were in place yet. With that in mind, I proactively defined success metrics to ensure the design’s impact could be rigorously evaluated once the product was ready to ship:

At Launch

Evaluate initial user engagement and identify early signals of whether the design successfully delivered value through the core workflow.

Activation Rate
Whether users completed their first storyboard, indicating the onboarding was intuitive enough to reach the key value moment.
Completion Rate
Whether users followed through the full pipeline from script to slideshow, reflecting end-to-end usability and perceived value.
Export Rate
Whether the rendered storyboard was compelling enough for users to act on — an early signal of output satisfaction.

Over Time

Assess sustained behavioral engagement and validate both the design strategy and its long-term business impact.

Feature Return Rate
Whether users consistently returned, indicating the workflow delivered sustained utility beyond initial curiosity.
Persona-Split Engagement
Whether the two user groups defined exhibited distinct usage patterns, validating the two-segment strategy and informing future prioritization.
Trial-to-Paid Conversion Rate
Whether perceived value was strong enough to drive upgrade decisions, directly linking design quality to revenue.

The strategic framing held up — storytelling support, human-AI collaboration, distinct user segments all proved directionally correct. What didn’t survive was the assumption that those insights would translate into a craft-first feature. The company made a different bet, two years later, for defensible reasons.

Insight #1
Competitive Repositioning

With startups like Runway and Pika earning early traction, and giants like Adobe and CapCut owning strong expertise, it is more prudent to strengthen existing revenue drivers than to compete in a vertical the company had no structural advantage in and no urgent need to win.

Insight #2
Narrative Reframing

Positioning as an autonomous agent rather than a smarter storyboard editor, and building a new suite rather than adding a tab, commanded a higher valuation multiple and aligned with where AI product discourse was heading in 2024–2025, as expected of a Series B company.

Takeaways

Product decisions and priorities are rarely decided by design alone; business stage, investor narrative, and organizational trust all shape what gets built and how, making proactive alignment and stakeholder visibility not a soft skill, but a strategic one.

Insight #1
Stage-Driven Strategy

A pre-Series A startup validating a concept operates under fundamentally different constraints than a post-Series A company racing for market share — the same feature can warrant entirely different design directions depending on where a company stands in its growth cycle.

Insight #2
Trust-Building Method

Establishing credibility within the team early is what drives iterative direction and secures alignment. When direct peer support is absent, maintaining momentum means putting something tangible in front of stand-by colleagues to keep feedback flowing.
