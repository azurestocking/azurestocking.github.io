Part 01

Overview

Opus Clip was an expanding Pre-Series A startup specializing in AI-powered short video repurposing and captioning, now aiming to integrate a text-to-video generation feature into its current product amid limited resources and intensifying market.

Our research yielded data-driven insights through iterative prototype-based testing, uncovering diverse user segments and their varied expectations and shared high-level goals, culminating in a design proposal for an end-to-end, human-AI collaborative storytelling support tool.

Background

Amidst the AIGC boom, integrating a text-to-video feature seemed timely and feasible. Yet, this loosely defined but potentially valuable opportunity needed to be validated and conceptualized, quickly and affordably, before securing leadership buy-in and diverting core production efforts.

Background #1
Market Interest

The recent advancement of generative AI has fueled momentum in multimodal creation, with text-to-video emerging as a compelling frontier. Startups like Pika and Runway signal both technical feasibility and strong market demand.

Background #2
Existential Crisis

The client is among the earliest players in AI-powered video captioning and repurposing, but the low barrier to entry makes differentiation essential to maintain its competitive edge as more industry giants enter the space.

Background #3
Potential Synergy

Our existing users have been requesting text-to-video generation feature, which complements the current focus on captioning and repurposing by extending the workflow earlier in the pipeline.

Challenges

As an external consultant, I was brought in to lead a focused rapid prototyping sprint under low internal priority. The go-to-market challenge lies not in design implementation alone, but also in clarifying underlying strategic ambiguity.

Challenge #1
Limited Resource

The company was an early-stage startup preparing for Series A, operating with a small team and limited budget, and lacking the bandwidth to make a full-time hire for temporary concept testing.

Challenge #2
Twofold Ambiguity

Our research goal is to strategically validate this opportunity by defining a compelling value proposition and identifying key user segments, and technically, to align the feature with actual user workflows in design.

Design Process

We adopted a Lean approach to move quickly and stay focused on user needs, using iterative prototyping to validate assumptions and evaluate outcomes, thereby enabling confident product direction without overcommitting resources.

Highlights

Proposed an end-to-end storytelling support tool for AI video production, built on a multistage generative media pipeline focused on human-AI collaborative workflow enhancement rather than dependence on powerful models, enabling faster iteration, consistent output, and flexible control.

Part 02

Think, Make, Check

Starting with a hypothesis: leading players appeared to focus on generating impressive but disjointed video clips, placing less emphasis on connecting them into structured narrative, which positions themselves more as tools for visual experimentation and asset creation.

Market Analysis

I identified several leading players along the production cycle and established a set of criteria to assess their narrative capabilities.

The matrix reveals a strategic opportunity: to differentiate by centering on storytelling support, aligning with the market gap and the team’s strengths, instead of competing on model performance.

Brainstorm

We brainstormed features that support storytelling more directly than model-centric competitors, using a mind map. Those deemed technically feasible and within design scope were marked as low-hanging fruits.

Wireframe

We explored how to organize the feature set through concrete interface paradigms and interaction patterns, forming two different directions.

Option #1
Storyboard

A visual tool used by filmmakers and animators to pre-visualize and plan a production clearly and efficiently, e.g., Toon Boom Storyboard.

Option #2
Infinite Canvas

A modular approach used by domain experts to design and execute complex systems visually, e.g., Grasshopper, Unreal Engine.

Concept Testing

Although the node-based option appeared intriguing, particularly for designers and engineers, I facilitated a cross-functional stakeholder interview to evaluate its viability, walking through annotated sketches and assessing each concept’s strengths, limitations, and strategic fit.

Part 03

Think, Make, Check +1

With stakeholder feedback in hand, the next step was to synthesize insights and determine the stronger path forward before moving into the next round of iteration.

Data Analysis

Grouping feedback into thematic categories, the affinity mapping revealed that implementing a node-based architecture would require substantial engineering refactoring, raising critical concerns across technical, usability, and strategic dimensions.

Insight #1
Technical Complexity

Requires each cell to be developed standalone yet communicative, and the magnitude of potential cell combinations exponentially poses challenges in scalability, agility, and version control.

Insight #2
Usability Uncertainty

Introduces flexibility with larger possibility of choice paralysis and increased cognitive load, which may alienate users with a fragmented, unpredictable, and delayed experience.

Insight #3
Managerial Misalignment

Demands significant resources in labor, capital, and time for development and operation, potentially resulting in delays, shifts, or failures in achieving long-term strategic goals.

Given these constraints and an unfavorable input-output ratio, we deprioritized this direction and redirected focus toward the storyboard alternative.

Prototype

Introduced an intermediary step of text-to-image generation to stabilize the creative workflow, enabling users to preview keyframes and iterate on their sequence, timing, and narrative logic in areas where models typically fall short.

Prompt

Enrich the user input with storytelling techniques to produce a complete, engaging screenplay.

Screenplay

Parse the screenplay into shot-by-shot scripts with structured metadata to guide text-to-image generation.

Storyboard

Tweak keyframes, voiceover, and animation settings to better align with the user’s creative intent.

Slideshow

Simulate the structure and pacing of the final video to reduce uncertainty in image-to-video generation.

Usability Testing

To ensure comprehensive representation across hypothesized user segments, I recruited a slightly larger sample of 8 participants, complemented by a screener survey to understand how each might incorporate the feature into their existing workflow.

Sessions employed the think-aloud protocol to surface key usability and functionality issues in real time. Feedback was then synthesized through affinity mapping, grouped thematically and color-coded by usage intensity.

Part 04

Think, Make, Check +2

Digging deeper, we found that feedback converged on the same areas for improvement, yet diverged sharply on how to address them, which raises a more fundamental question: Why are users holding conflicting opinions on the same areas for improvement?

Data Analysis

To make sense of those seemingly irreconcilable contradictions, I first examined the underlying intent behind each request, and then reshuffled them by high-level goals—the broader needs encompassing possibly divergent user behaviors and expectations.

The affinity mapping indicates that while user expectations diverge, they fall under the same high-level goals, implying that the divergence stems from how users conceptualize these goals differently.

Goal #1
Efficiency

Reduce time, effort, financial cost, and cognitive friction by providing readable information, an intuitive interface, and a streamlined, user-friendly workflow.

Goal #2
Consistency

Enable a coherent narrative and stable visual identity across frames, while allowing reasonable variation to support continuity in action, time, and space.

Goal #3
Adaptability

Support varied intents and use cases through rich customization options, flexible control over outputs and workflows, and a context-aware, responsive system.

Problem Statement

We began with a straightforward yet knowingly simplistic problem framing based on client’s request. With the emerged high-level goals, I refined that rough question:

How might we empower content creators to quickly and easily translate their scripts into reliably high-quality videos while accommodating diverse scenarios?

Data Analysis

The observation that users conceptualize high-level goals differently validates our hypothesis of multiple user segments. This divergence indicates that distinct user groups inevitably adopt varying positions on value trade-offs, which was expected to be evident through their clustering along axes representing two core tensions in users’ decision-making.

Axis #1
Cost vs. Perceived Quality

This axis plots users’ expectations for production by reflecting their weighting of resources commitment against the value of outcomes. Dimensions highlighted in feedback include time-saved, effort-saved, and craftsmanship, with the extremes of this axis representing cost and perceived quality.

Axis #2
User Agency vs. Automation

This axis plots users’ preferences for interaction by reflecting their willingness to delegate creative control to the system. Dimensions highlighted in feedback include authorship, precision, and freedom, with the extremes of this axis representing user agency versus automation.

Mapping all sticky notes onto these two axes allowed us to reveal the logically inferred user segmentation.

Sticky notes were distributed diagonally from the upper-left to lower-right quadrants, with high and low usage intensity clustering at opposite ends, demonstrating that:

Insight #1
We Don’t Trust AI

The negative correlation between perceived quality and automation validates the hypothesis that users associate strong outputs with manual control, implying limited trust in fully automated systems to deliver professional results.

Insight #2
User Segmentation

The polarization by usage intensity reveals two distinct user groups: those with lower intensity favoring low cost and automation, and others with higher intensity prioritizing user agency and quality.

Personae

Based on user segmentation, I developed two personas. Original creators are typically users with a high level of creative expertise, such as indie filmmakers and animators. Derivative creators are more likely users with distribution-driven priorities, including influencers and content aggregators.

Persona #1
Original Creator

Seek to maintain creative authorship while achieving high-quality outputs; cautious of full automation and perceive the tool as an extension.

Persona #2
Derivative Creator

Aim to produce acceptable outputs with minimal input; willing to delegate complex tasks to the system and view the tool as a replacement.

Prototype

Improved clarity and workflow efficiency through both structural and functional enhancements, including a redesigned layout, surfaced global settings, and centralized asset control to support faster, more consistent generation.

Iteration #1

Layout Efficiency

BEFORE

Arranged shots horizontally, causing confusion due to misalignment between the container and slider lengths, which conflicted with standard scrolling behavior.

AFTER

Switched to a vertical layout with two modes, and collapsed shot editing options with hover effects to reduce cognitive load through prioritized information display.

Iteration #2

Customization Consistency Adaptability

BEFORE

Required manual configuration of global settings each time via a low-discoverability button, leading to repetitive setup and frequent oversights.

AFTER

Displayed global settings in a prominent sidebar and introduced both predefined and customizable templates to streamline setup and ensure consistency.

BEFORE

Required extensive manual effort to tweak shots individually in order to maintain visual consistency across shots.

AFTER

Extracted shared assets from the script automatically, enabling centralized control for easy insertion and batch-level tweaking.

Iteration #3

Inline Editing Efficiency Consistency

BEFORE

Applied AI-assisted text enhancements to the entire text area, altering unrelated content and lacking support for targeted control.

AFTER

Introduced a floating toolbar that allows users to select specific text segments for focused, instruction-based modifications in the editor.

Iteration #4

Separate Workflow Adaptability

BEFORE

Followed a linear path with a fixed entry point, assuming all users start from scratch and proceed through every editing stage.

AFTER

Added conditional branching and early skip options to bypass unnecessary editing steps based on user readiness, reducing the minimum required steps.

Usability Testing

The updated prototype underwent testing with 5 additional participants, and their feedback was grouped by thematic categories to identify areas for improvement.

Part 05

Think, Make, Check +3

Data Analysis

However, when I reclassified the requests using the previously found high-level goals, several items didn’t fit any known category, surfacing an unstated goal.

Even with their diverse focuses, the uncategorized feedback unanimously converged on a previously unmentioned dimension: transparency—a deeper understanding of the system’s operations and decisions, and hence more informed and targeted manipulation.

Insight #1
Processing Logic Exposure

Demystify how inputs and parameters are interpreted, e.g., establish a visually clear cause-effect relationship between script segments and corresponding generated shots.

Insight #2
Cognitive Load Distribution

Offer progressive disclosure of information to guide user interactions, e.g., simplify complex, effort-intensive prompting by disassembling it into smaller, manageable pieces via structured input fields.

Insight #3
Human-in-the-Loop

Integrate deliberate checkpoints where users can validate, revise, or override AI outputs, e.g., include undo/redo capabilities and history log to revert states or track changes.

Prototype

We included transparency as a new evaluation lens while continuing to improve core usability through fine-grained refinements, including domain-specific text enhancement, clarified interface hierarchy and interaction logic, more and informative iteration support.

Iteration #1

Script Reformatting Efficiency Consistency

BEFORE

Relied on plain text with minimal markdown-style grammar, confusing non-technical users and necessitating model’s semantic understanding.

AFTER

Added a reformat option to declare key elements with symbolic tags, improving frontend styling and generative performance.

BEFORE

Displayed all options as buttons with icons and text labels, and provided sliders to adjust tone and length, resulting in redundant choices and unnecessary clicks.

AFTER

Replaced label-heavy buttons with grouped icon buttons, differentiated prompt input from function controls, and removed redundant two-step interactions.

Iteration #2

Shot Editing Consistency

BEFORE

Misarranged controls with an illogical information architecture, juxtaposed unrelated functions and nested options that should have remained parallel.

AFTER

Reorganized controls by target objects and logical relationships across the floating toolbar and modal, and removed redundant buttons.

BEFORE

The trigger button and output keyframe appeared on separate pages with no visual continuity, breaking the traceable connection between input and result.

AFTER

Flattened the page hierarchy using modals and in-textarea dropdowns to establish a more intuitive cause-and-effect relationship.

BEFORE

Generated audio tracks exclusively from voiceover input, limiting applicability to genres like vlogs and commentary where narrative closely follows the visuals.

AFTER

Separated dialogue from voiceover to support formats like feature films, where audio often diverges from visuals and voiceover alone is insufficient.

Iteration #3

Preview Transparency

BEFORE

Allowed preview only at the final step via an ambiguously labeled button, with no option to set starting points, discouraging early identification of issues.

AFTER

Introduced a slideshow mode that simulates the final output’s pacing and structure before committing, with fluid switching between modes.

Iteration #4

Version Control Transparency

BEFORE

Kept a flat version control system that recorded all generation variations, offering little value for tracking meaningful changes or refining prompts.

AFTER

Maintained an iteration log that highlights differences compared to the last selected version, enabling more informed and focused revisions.

Success Metrics

Full implementation would be a major, resource-intensive undertaking requiring leadership buy-in and broader organizational alignment—none of which were in place yet. With that in mind, I proactively defined success metrics to ensure the design's impact could be rigorously evaluated when the product was ready to ship:

At Launch

Evaluate initial user engagement and identify early signals of whether the design successfully delivered value through the core workflow.

Activation Rate
Evaluate whether the onboarding experience successfully guided users to complete their first storyboard, indicating whether the core workflow was intuitive enough to reach the key value moment.
Completion Rate
Measure whether users followed through the full pipeline from script to slideshow preview, reflecting the end-to-end flow's usability and perceived value.
Export Rate
Identify early signals of output satisfaction, reflecting whether the rendered storyboard was compelling enough for users to act on.

Over Time

Assess sustained behavioral engagement and validate both the design strategy and its long-term business impact.

Feature Return Rate
Measure whether users consistently returned to the storyboard feature, indicating that the human-AI collaborative workflow delivered sustained utility beyond initial curiosity.
Persona-Split Engagement
Track whether Original Creators and Derivative Creators exhibited distinct usage patterns, validating the two-segment design strategy and informing future prioritization.
Trial-to-Paid Conversion Rate
Assess whether the feature's perceived value was strong enough to drive upgrade decisions, directly linking UX quality to revenue potential.
Part 06

Retrospect

The company has successfully raised $30M in Series A in August 2024 and $20M in Series B in March 2025, but ultimately shipped a fully automated, standalone video generation product rather than the human-collaborative, integrated feature I proposed. Why?

Hindsights

The major differences in design and business strategy reflect a strategic pivot toward a broader, lower-friction market amid intensifying competition, trading craft and control for speed, scale, and a stronger investor narrative.

Difference #1

Automation-First System Efficiency Scalability

BEFORE

Proposed a human-AI collaborative workspace with deliberate checkpoints for users to inspect, refine, and override outputs at each production stage.

AFTER

Demonstrated high autonomy through its multi-agent system that handles complex creative workflows with minimal manual intervention.

Difference #2

Opinionated Target User Efficiency Scalability

BEFORE

Served two distinct personas: the original creator who demands authorship and precision, and the derivative creator who prioritizes speed and volume.

AFTER

Stripped back intermediate creative handles to optimize for faceless creators who want publishable output with minimal creative investment.

Difference #3

Independent Product Line Scalability

BEFORE

Assumed text-to-video would be integrated as a tab within the core product, pulling the workflow earlier into the production pipeline.

AFTER

Launched as an entirely separate product with its own domain, branding, and entrance, rather than a feature increment.

The strategic framing held up—storytelling support, human-AI collaboration, distinct user segments all proved directionally correct. What didn’t survive was the assumption that those insights would translate into a craft-first feature. The company made a different bet, two years later, for defensible reasons.

Reason #1
Competitive Repositioning

With startups like Runway and Pika earning early traction, and giants like Adobe and CapCut owning strong expertise, it is more prudent to strengthen existing revenue drivers than to compete in a vertical the company had no structural advantage in and no urgent need to win.

Reason #2
Narrative Reframing

Positioning as an autonomous agent rather than a smarter storyboard editor, and building a new suite rather than adding a tab, commanded a higher valuation multiple and aligned with where AI product discourse was heading in 2024–2025, as expected of a Series B company.

Reason #3
Cannibalization Risk

The core tool repurposes existing footage while the new tool generates footage from scratch, serving fundamentally different jobs-to-be-done with incompatible mental models. Folding them into one product would have blurred both value propositions at a stage when the core business still needed to consolidate.

Takeaways

Product decisions and priorities are rarely decided by design alone; business stage, investor narrative, and organizational trust all shape what gets built and how, making proactive alignment and stakeholder visibility not a soft skill, but a strategic one.

Takeaway #1
Business stage dictates priorities

A pre-Series A startup validating a concept has different constraints than a post-Series A company racing to capture market share. The same feature can warrant completely different design directions depending on where the company is in its growth cycle.

Takeaway #2
Investor narrative shapes decisions

The shift from an integrated tab to a standalone product, and from collaborative to autonomous, reflects fundraising logic as much as user research. At growth stage, product and pitch deck are inseparable.

Takeaway #3
Trust Building

As an external consultant with limited visibility and resources, establishing credibility within the team early is what drives iterative direction and secures alignment. When direct peer support is absent, maintaining momentum means putting something tangible in front of stand-by colleagues to keep feedback flowing.
